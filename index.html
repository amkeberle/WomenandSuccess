Women and Success

STEM Field

Elizabeth Blackwell (Medicine)

Elizabeth Blackwell has made great contributions to the field of medicine in the United States. Becoming the first woman in America to receive a doctor of medicine, Blackwell devoted her life to focus on women and children in medicine, eventually establishing the New York Infirmary for Women and Children in 1857.

Chien-Shiung Wu (Physics)

Known as the “First Lady of Physics,” Wu has immersed herself in the study of physics. She was a member of the Manhattan project in which she collaborated with others to create the atomic bombs used in the Nagasaki and Hiroshima bombing. Later, Wu and two male colleagues disproved a law of symmetry in physics called the “principle of conservation of parity.

Radia Perlman (Computer Scientist)

Being an MIT graduate in the 60s, Perlman is known for her creation of the Spanning Tree Protocol (SPI) that allowed the internet to become possible. She changed the Ethernet from a single wired, CSMA/CD into a large-scale protocol.

Elizabeth Blackburn (Biology)

Blackburn has received a Nobel prize for her work in biology. She was the first Australian woman to be awarded the Nobel prize She was awarded the Nobel prize for discovering telomerase, an enzyme that replenishes telomeres. Her work has extended into other areas such as analyzing telomerase and telomeres' relationship to mental health and physical health. Blackburn now continues as a researcher at the University of San Francisco, studying the impacts of stress on telomerase.

History + Literature

Virginia Woolf

Virginia Woolf is known for being a modern writer at the time who challenged social injustices for women in the 1900s. She later came on to open the Hogarth publishing house where many of her works were published.

Jane Austen

Known for her writings in Pride and Prejudice and Emma, Austen has achieved many in her career as a writer. Her writings vividly depicted English middle-class life during the 19th century. Years later, Austen’s works have been transformed into films and integrated as conversations within the literature.

Margaret Atwood

Margaret Atwood is most known for her novel The Handmaiden’s Tale. Her novel depicts an entirely new world that is set in New England in the near future that depicts a Christian fundamentalist theocratic regime in the former U.S. that arose as a response to a fertility crisis. She has won many awards for her work including the booker award and a nebula award.
